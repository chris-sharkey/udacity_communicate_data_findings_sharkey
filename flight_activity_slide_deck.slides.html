Flight Activity¶

by Chris Sharkey¶

Investigation Overview¶

2020 has been, in a word, "unprecedented". One of the industries hit the hardest by the COVID-19 pandemic is the airline industry. I was curious about the similarities and differences between the current crisis and the September 11th World Trade Center attacks in 2001.

Dataset Overview¶

I pulled two months of flight activity data from the US Bureau of Transportation Statistics. The first month is from September 2001 and the second month is from March of 2020.

Flights Vs Cancellations in September 2001¶

We are going to start by exploring the September 2001 data. Let's start by looking at the number of flights vs the number of cancellations during the month. Notice how there is a huge spike in cancellations with all flights being cancelled on September 12th.

Distribution of Departure Delays During the Week of September 9th¶

Now that we've seen the trends over the course of the month, let's focus on the week of September 9th. During this week, there was great volatility in the overall trend of departure delays. Notice how on the 9th and the 10th, there is a very tight distribution of departure delays around 0. Any negative departure delays mean that the flight left the gate early. Even though there is usually some skew to having longer delays due to a few outliers, on September 13th, 14, and 15th, there was far greater skew. There were no flights on September 12th.

Conclusion¶

In conclusion, the crises of September 11, 2001 and the COVID-19 pandemic are two very different crises with different time horizons and different effects on the airline industry. While the September 11th crisis had a sudden and sharp impact on flight activity, the number of flights rebounded quickly. The COVID-19 pandemic, however, has long-lasting consequences for the airline industry. Even now, in October 2020, flight activities are still lower than the were a year ago. Even a predictive analysis (while interesting) could not tell us exactly how long this could last.
